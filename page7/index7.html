We’ve Completed the Manchester Textile Mill Transformation

Last month, our bureau finalised the adaptive reuse of a 19th‑century textile mill in Manchester’s Ancoats district. The 8,000 m² building now houses a co‑working hub, a café, and 12 affordable artist studios. We preserved the original cast‑iron columns, exposed brickwork, and timber roof trusses, while installing a ground‑source heat pump and triple‑glazed windows. The project achieved BREEAM Excellent and is already 90% leased. Local heritage groups praised our approach, and the city council plans to use it as a case study for future regeneration. We’ll host an open day on 15 July for the public to explore the space.

Top Architectural Trends Shaping 2026 (Analytical)

This year, three forces redefine architecture: climate urgency, digital innovation, and social hybridity. Biophilic design is now mandatory in most public buildings – we integrate living walls, daylight harvesting, and natural ventilation from the first sketch. Parametric modelling and AI allow us to generate ultra‑efficient structures, but we always balance them with human scale and tactile materials. Net‑zero energy is the baseline; many clients now ask for energy‑positive buildings that feed surplus power to the grid. Flexibility remains critical – movable partitions and multi‑use zones let spaces evolve with changing needs. These trends are not fleeting; they signal a permanent shift toward responsive, regenerative architecture.

Our Bristol Community Centre Wins National Sustainability Award

We are thrilled to announce that the Bristol Community Centre, completed in late 2025, has won the UK Green Building Council’s “Project of the Year” award. The judging panel praised its integrated approach: a cross‑laminated timber frame, a sedum green roof that manages 80% of stormwater, and a natural clay plaster system that regulates indoor humidity. The centre also features a community orchard and a rainwater harvesting tank that supplies toilets and irrigation. Since opening, the building has hosted over 200 events and reduced its operational carbon by 65% compared to a conventional design. The award ceremony takes place next week in London, and we will share more details on our social channels.

Sustainable Architecture – Beyond Green Labels (Analytical)

Genuine sustainability goes far beyond certificates. We start with passive design: orientation, shading, and thermal mass cut energy demand by up to 40% for free. Then we select low‑embodied‑carbon materials – CLT, recycled steel, and hempcrete – all sourced within 100 miles of the site. Water is precious: we install greywater recycling and permeable paving on every project. But the real test is longevity – we design for disassembly, so components can be reused or recycled decades later. Healthy indoor air, acoustics, and connection to nature are non‑negotiable. Only when environmental, social, and economic factors align can we call a building truly sustainable.

We’ve Launched a New Smart‑Building Consultancy Service

Starting this June, our bureau offers a dedicated “Intelligent Spaces” service to help clients integrate smart technologies from the earliest design stage. The service covers sensor networks for occupancy and air quality, automated shading and lighting controls, and predictive maintenance systems that reduce operational costs by up to 25%. We’ve already piloted it on three residential projects, where residents reported improved comfort and 30% lower energy bills. To lead this initiative, we’ve hired Dr. Elena Rossi, a former researcher at the Bartlett School of Architecture. She will host a free webinar on 30 June to explain the benefits. We believe that smart design should be invisible – it should anticipate needs without overwhelming users.

Designing Public Spaces That Connect People (Analytical)

Great public spaces are not accidents – they are carefully choreographed. We design squares, parks, and plazas with layered seating, varied sun/shade patterns, and water features that invite play and relaxation. Pedestrian priority and bike lanes are integrated from the start, not as an afterthought. Flexibility is key: a single square can host a farmers’ market, an open‑air cinema, or a quiet reading area depending on the time of day. We also involve local communities through workshops to understand their daily rituals. The result is a place where people linger, chat, and return – a true social condenser. Our recent redesign of a neglected plaza in Leeds saw a 200% increase in footfall within three months.
